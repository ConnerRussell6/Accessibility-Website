About Me

When I Hear "Accessibility"

When I hear accessibility I hear a need to design carefully and thoughtfully. Asscessibility and especially web accessibility has come a long way but we are not there yet. I want to be able to improve and provide accessible websites and designs to further advance the technologies effectiveness in this area. By following accessibility standards, websites can enhance user experience, foster inclusivity, and contribute to a more equitable digital landscape. The ongoing efforts to promote and prioritize web accessibility are esstential for building a digital world that truly serves the needs of all individuals.

Automated Tools Are Not Enough

Relying solely on automated testing has several drawbacks. Automated tests may not capture nuanced issues detectable by human testers, such as visual inconsistencies or complex user experience challenges. They also have limitations in uncovering unforeseen scenarios or edge cases, as they focus on predefined test cases. Maintenance of automated tests can be demanding, requiring frequent updates to keep pace with evolving software. Moreover, automated testing cannot replace the intuition and adaptability of human testers during exploratory testing, making a balanced approach, combining both automated and manual testing, essential for comprehensive quality assurance.

Accessibility Standards I Use

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG):
WCAG is a widely recognized set of guidelines developed by the Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C). These guidelines outline principles and success criteria for making web content more accessible to people with disabilities. They cover areas such as perceivability, operability, understandability, and robustness.
ARIA (Accessible Rich Internet Applications):
ARIA is a set of attributes that can be added to HTML elements to enhance the accessibility of dynamic content and web applications. It provides additional information to assistive technologies, helping them better interpret and convey the purpose and state of interactive elements.
Mobile Accessibility Standards:
Mobile accessibility standards focus on ensuring that mobile applications and websites are usable by individuals with disabilities. These standards often align with WCAG but may include specific considerations for mobile interfaces, touch interactions, and screen sizes.
Usability and User-Centered Design:
While not specific to accessibility, incorporating usability and user-centered design principles is integral to creating an accessible experience. Considering user needs, conducting usability testing, and obtaining feedback from individuals with disabilities are essential aspects of creating truly accessible digital products.

Why Inclusion During Design Matters

Including all people in testing and design processes is essential for achieving accessibility goals. Diverse perspectives, stemming from individuals with various abilities and backgrounds, bring a broader range of insights, uncovering potential accessibility issues that might be overlooked by a more homogenous group. User-centered design is enhanced when designers understand the needs of the entire user base, ensuring that interfaces are intuitive and usable for everyone. Direct engagement with individuals with disabilities in usability testing provides real-world feedback, revealing how users navigate interfaces and uncovering any challenges they may encounter. Inclusive design not only supports legal compliance but also reflects ethical considerations, promoting equal access to information and services for all individuals.
